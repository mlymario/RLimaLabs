Microfluidic clinical tools and blood flow at a microscale level:

In 2005, at Tohoku University (Japan), RL used a clean room facility (class 1000) to develop microfluidic devices, made of polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS), suitable for a confocal micro-PIV/PTV system. By using this system, he discovered unique hemodynamic phenomena and used these findings to make valuable and unique clinical tools able to perform multiple tasks in one single device.


Some selected publications about unique microscale level phenomena:




1. By using a confocal micro-PIV it was possible for the first time to investigate 3D blood flow.

Phenomena happening in microfluidic devices.

More details at:

https://doi.org/10.1007/s10544-007-9121-z

https://doi.org/10.1088/0957-0233/17/4/026




2. By using a confocal micro-PTV system, a new protocol was developed to label blood cells by using a lipophilic carbocyanime derivative, to perform micro-PTV measurements. By using this methodology, it was possible for the first time to visualize and measure not only the interactions of individual red blood cells (RBCs) but also both translation and rotational motion of RBCs.

More details can be found at:

https://doi.org/10.1007/s10439-009-9732-z

https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jbiomech.2008.04.033




3. By using a high-speed video microscopy system, RL discovered that at the centre of complex micronetworks cell free layers (CFLs) can be formed. Before, the scientific community believed that CFLs only exist around the microhannels walls.

https://doi.org/10.1063/1.3672689

https://doi.org/10.1016/j.expthermflusci.2019.109847

Some selected prototypes and clinical tools are the following:


1. CSD-chip: A Continuous flow microfluidic device for RBC Separation and Deformability assessment, first proposed in 2011 and in 2019 was successfully tested with fifteen diabetic type II patients from Bragança Hospital, Portugal.

https://doi.org/10.1007/s13206-013-7408-0

https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mne.2022.100149



2. RBChyperbolic-chip: A microfluidic device to assess RBCs deformability under a controlled extensional flow, first proposed in 2012 and successfully tested with 20 end-stage kidney disease patients from Gondomar Hemodialysis Clinic, Portugal. The effect of nanocarriers on the RBCs mechanical deformability was also assessed. It was proved that this clinical tool has a higher sensitivity in comparison with traditional hemolysis methods.

https://doi.org/10.3390/mi10100645
https://doi.org/10.1007/s11051-016-3498-7

Nanofluids, thermofluids and blood analogues:

Nanofluids (NFs) are a new class of thermofluids that have a great potential to improve heat transfer systems. However, one main limitation is the long-term stability of nanoparticles (NPs). Being the PI of the FCT project “NFsCoolingSystem” and in collaboration with INL, it was developed a novel and extremely stable NF based on iron oxide NPs. In the near future, it is expected to test this NF at different kinds of heat transfer systems.

https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tsep.2021.101085



Handling real blood is not straightforward and has several complex problems and difficulties, such as sanitary and physiological issues. Hence, RL have developed different kinds of blood analogues that mimics the real one such as:

1. Particulate Blood Analogue (PBA) based on flexible PDMS microparticles made by a multi-stage membrane emulsification process, 2021;

https://doi.org/10.1039/D1SM00106J

2. PBA based on Brij L4 micelles, 2020;

https://doi.org/10.1007/s00348-020-03066-7

3. PBA based on flexible PDMS microparticles made by a flow focusing technique, 2016 to 2019;

https://doi.org/10.1063/1.4943007

From microfluidic devices to organs-on-a-chip:




In 2018, with the collaboration of the Microelectronics group (UMinho), INL and other international labs., RL et al. decided to combine biomaterials, cell culture, and advanced microfluidic technologies to develop organ-on-a-chip platforms able to reproduce the physiological microenvironment of human organs and eventually in the future replace animal models. Hence, an international patent (WO2022/180595) was obtained from this research and very recently a paper was published at the Comput.
